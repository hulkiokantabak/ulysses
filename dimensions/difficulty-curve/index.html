The Difficulty Curve

Dimension 11 · Type III, Case study (primary evidence from one reader) · Build status: building · Panel score: not yet scored

This dimension is being built now.

First in the build order, now first built to its HTML and diagram registers. The document PDF and the deck are the registers that remain.

The claim
Where a first reader of Ulysses actually struggles is measurable from a complete dated record of one accompanied read — and it does not match the folklore. The difficulty is real, but it moves: period reference in the front half, form in the middle, voice and referent at the end; and question density tracks engagement at least as much as struggle.
The unit
the logged question — 290 extracted, 245 placed against episodes, page-anchored across sixteen exchanges of an accompanied first read
What it shows
This reader did not break at Proteus, where folklore says readers break — episodes 2–3 drew zero placed questions. His hardest sustained stretch was Sirens through Oxen of the Sun (episodes 11–14), where the record's only survival language appears. The three most-questioned episodes — Circe (40), Penelope (38), Ithaca (33) — are the ones he loved loudest and read fastest: the curve of questions is a curve of engagement wearing the costume of difficulty.

Sources

  • The Reading Companion is the sole source — primary evidence, no external material. The Proteus folklore and the Hawking Index are named as the claims under test, not used as data.

Confidence

n = 1, an accompanied read, stated in the document's own voice throughout (anchor A10). A question is not a unit of difficulty; the artifact's caveats section carries every known way the dataset can mislead, including four quote-verification failures out of 290 extractions and the undercounted early episodes.
