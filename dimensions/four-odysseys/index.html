Four Odysseys

Dimension 16 · Type IV, Comparative (the book against other versions of itself) · Build status: built · Panel score: not yet scored

Built to every register it budgets: the two-axis chart (diagram), the audience deck (presentation), and — 19 July 2026 — the document PDF. It was marked back from built to building earlier the same day (C-26), because its PDF register was being closed by a linear document made outside this project; that document is its SOURCE MATERIAL, filed under Reading, and the register is this one. Supplies dimension 9's analytic frame — refusing plot and refusing register are different acts.

The claim
Fidelity to Homer's plot and interior orientation are two separable axes, usually collapsed into one. Collapsed, the four Odysseys line up on a single scale of faithfulness; separated, they stop lining up at all.
The unit
the work, measured on two axes — fidelity to Homer's plot and genre, and interior orientation (Homer · Wilson 2017 · Nolan 2026 · Joyce 1922)
What it shows
Wilson and Joyce share an anti-mystification impulse — both remove something (archaism; heroic distance) rather than add, while Nolan adds. Wilson de-mystifies the sentence; Nolan de-mystifies the hero but keeps the myth; Joyce discards the myth's events and re-mystifies the ordinary. Three different kinds of empathy — not one scale of it. A sharper test of the two axes: at the hanging of the slave girls, Wilson refuses to look away and renders it flatly — checkable in her own book — while Nolan is reported to remove the scene altogether, displacing it onto a single betrayer. The same event, and the translator is the one who will not soften it. Note the asymmetry of the evidence: Wilson's half is verifiable, Nolan's half is second-hand.

Confidence

Every claim about Nolan's film is second-hand, and the later commentary does NOT corroborate the earlier handoff: both rest on day-one journalism about the same film, so agreement between them is not two witnesses. One correction to how this was first stated (C-30): the outlets named — Vanity Fair, GQ, TODAY.com — are the commentary's own; the handoff cites a different list, so what is demonstrated is an overlap in KIND of source rather than a shared citation. Nothing has been promoted on the grounds that two documents agree. Two further limits on the admitted commentary: its bibliography is a block list, so no individual claim in it is traceable to a particular source; and it presents nine departures where its own cited Vanity Fair piece names ten, so its selection is editorial. The one claim in it that would matter most — that Wilson has confirmed she was not consulted by Nolan — cannot be supported from its own citations, whose only Wilson interview predates the film by nine years; it is held at Medium permanently — the interview would have to exist and be found, and neither is this project's to pursue. The Wilson half of this dimension was checked against public web sources on 19 July 2026, after catch C-28 found it asserting a verification nobody had performed. The check was adversarial — a second pass instructed to break the first — and it changed the markings in both directions. **Nothing found is false; all three claims were overstated, each by one load-bearing word.** (1) THE LINE COUNT. That the translation has the same number of lines as the Greek is now **High**: Wilson's Translator's Note says so in her own words, W. W. Norton's book page repeats it, and independent readers of the printed book — Roberts in The Spenser Review, Burrow in the LRB, Miller in the Washington Post — confirm it. But the figure **12,110 is withdrawn as Unconfirmed**: no primary source, publisher, catalogue or review gives a number for the English. It is arithmetic inherited from a Wikipedia sentence, and the Greek total is itself edition-dependent — the Perseus text numbers to 12,110 but prints 12,107 verses. And **'line-for-line' is Wilson's own phrase for her working constraint, not a description of a strict mapping**: a reader comparing the texts finds her rendering of the tenth Greek line beginning on her ninth and ending on her eleventh. (2) THE METER. That it is unrhymed iambic pentameter, chosen as the English analogue to dactylic hexameter, is **High** and primary. **'Strict' is withdrawn** — it appears in no source of any class; Wilson's invariable word is *regular*, and the one critic who examines the meter closely (Armstrong, LARB 2018) describes her lines as flexible, notes eleven-syllable feminine endings, and contrasts her explicitly with Pope. Her own most-quoted line opens on a trochaic inversion. (3) 'SLAVE'. This is the best-evidenced claim and the one that matters most here. That the choice is deliberate is **High and primary**: the Translator's Note states that rendering a *dmoe* as 'maid' or 'domestic servant' 'would imply that she was free,' so she has 'often used slave.' That earlier translators avoided the word is **High and verified against the text** — the only place in this chain where printed evidence was checked rather than relayed: in Fagles's Books 21–22 'slave' appears zero times, against 'handmaids,' 'serving-women,' 'maids,' and Telemachus's 'You sluts — the suitors' whores!' But the implied uniform substitution is **Partial**: Wilson writes 'often,' concedes the word is less specific than the Greek's several terms, and — the sharpest finding — **in the Book 22 hanging, the very scene this dimension cites, she writes 'these girls' where the Greek has *dmoai*.** Anyone citing Book 22 as the proof text is citing the wrong passage; what it does support is the other half of this dimension's claim, that she refuses to look away — the line is 'I refuse to grant these girls a clean death.' Two figures are struck as untraceable: the 12,110 above, and a '173 versus 18' word-count that lives only in a single Quora answer. Independence: there are **two ancestors, not one** — Wilson's Translator's Note for everything about intent, and Wyatt Mason's November 2017 NYT Magazine profile for the numbers and the 'line for line' phrasing; Wilson restated across six venues is one witness speaking six times. **These markings are final.** The author has ruled that this project will not consult primary sources it does not hold: there is no library visit, no printed Norton edition, no screening of the film. That is a standing limit on the project, not a task deferred, and it is stated here so no future session records it as outstanding work. What the open web bears is what this dimension claims, and the markings above say exactly how much that is. Recorded as catches C-30 and C-31. **The four open questions about the film are CLOSED AS PERMANENTLY UNRESOLVED** (author's ruling, 19 July 2026, catch C-31): each would need the film itself, and this project does not consult sources it does not hold. They are kept here with their last known state rather than deleted, because an abandoned path is shown, not hidden (anchor A4). That state was: Calypso's arc reported as erasure then release — she gives the lotus so he forgets, and lets him go once he remembers what he is returning to — which narrowed the question without closing it; the Cattle of the Sun resolution and the Laestrygonian child's gender both thin; and whether Nolan has ever mentioned Joyce still unevidenced, the admitted commentary's assertion that he reads Homer through Joyce being the commentator's interpretation rather than a source, which closes nothing. Three departures reported late — Sinon imported from Virgil's Aeneid in place of Achilles, the elided hanging of the slave girls, and an ending in which Odysseus is exiled rather than celebrated — all push Nolan's fidelity toward the low edge of the stated ~70%; the marker was not moved, because second-hand evidence does not earn that precision, and it will not be moved now.
