2026-07-18 — Coverage survey: which dimensions are already taken

Purpose. Before adding two dimensions to the masters' ten, establish what the existing literature has already done properly, so the additions are genuine gaps.

Method. Web search across scholarly and general sources. Not exhaustive; sufficient to rule candidates in or out.

Ruled OUT — already well covered

Candidate Why ruled out Key evidence
Smell / the olfactory Substantial literature plus public projects Laura Frost and Christine O'Neill on Joyce and odour; multiple theses; 2026 Irish Times piece on perfumer Meabh Mc Curtin recreating 14 scents from the novel with O'Neill; a VR/scent performance (Go to Blazes, Ulysses 2.2)
Error and misquotation Dedicated essay collection plus a modern annotation edition Errears and Erroriboose: Joyce and Error (Brill) — first major collection on the topic; Annotations to James Joyce's Ulysses (Slote, Mamigonian, Turner, OUP 2022) accounts for errors line by line; Colm Toibin, "Arruginated: James Joyce's Errors," LRB 45.17 (2023)
Animals / the nonhuman Two special issues plus a prior tabulation Humanities special issue "Joyce, Animals and the Nonhuman" (2017); James Joyce Quarterly special issue; Robert Haas's tabulation of "over a thousand animal images" in Ulysses

Finding. Almost every content angle is taken. The openings are in method, and in what this particular reader is uniquely positioned to do.

Ruled IN — dimension 11, The Difficulty Curve

The abandonment of Ulysses is globally famous and almost entirely unstudied.

  • The best-known metric is the "Hawking Index," invented in 2014 by University of Wisconsin-Madison mathematician Jordan Ellenberg, using the position of popular Kindle highlights as a proxy for where readers stop. Explicitly unscientific by its own framing. (Money, "Sorry James Joyce, the People Buying Ulysses Don't Actually Read It")
  • The claim that most readers break at Proteus circulates as classroom folklore — e.g. Slant Books, "Bloomsday: How Ulysses Changes How We Read," relaying a professor's assertion that most readers will not get past the first paragraph of episode 3. Attributed to no study.
  • General reader-abandonment data exists (around 29% of readers do not finish most books they start) but is not book-specific.
  • Kiberd, Ulysses and Us argues for the common reader's claim on the book but measures nothing.
  • A large guide and annotation industry is premised on reader difficulty; there is no study of the difficulty itself.

Conclusion. A genuine gap, and one where this project holds primary data no one else has: a complete dated log of one first reading with page-anchored questions at the exact points of strain, and the interventions that resolved them. Case study, n = 1, stated.

Ruled IN — dimension 12, Ulysses in Turkish

Turkish has four versions and a live dispute; almost none of it is visible in anglophone Joyce studies.

Version Translator Year Publisher
Partial — Penelope only Murat Belge 1965 Yeni Dergi, no. 8
First complete Nevzat Erkmen 1996 Yapi Kredi
Companion volume Erkmen, Ulysses Sozlugu 2006 Yapi Kredi
Retranslation Armagan Ekici 2012 Norgunk
Retranslation Fuat Sevimay 2019 Kafka Kitap
  • Ekici's stated position (in Retranslating Joyce for the 21st Century, Brill 2020, ch. 9): partly because of Erkmen's translation strategies, Ulysses is still known to most Turkish readers as cold and unreadable, impenetrably experimental and erudite. He retranslated to make Turkish readers feel the richness, humanity and humour, and to recreate his own joy in reading the original.
  • He works from Fritz Senn's warning that translators fall for the lure of grammatical rectification and flatten out the essential strangeness of Joyce's text.
  • Existing comparative work is thin. Ekici's own Brill chapter compares his version to Erkmen's. One journal article (RumeliDE, 2020) compares Belge / Erkmen / Ekici on Penelope. Sevimay's 2019 version is barely represented in the comparative literature.
  • Turkish reader discourse is active and contested (Eksi Sozluk, Goodreads Turkish reading groups): Erkmen described as dictionary-driven and Ottoman-inflected, harder than the original; Ekici as the readable one; Ekici has publicly criticised Sevimay's Turkish and called Erkmen's Sozlugu plagiarism.
  • Erkmen died in 2020.

Conclusion. No systematic four-way comparison on a common set of cruxes exists; almost nothing is available in English; the Turkish reception is effectively absent from the field. Uniquely available to a bilingual Turkish/English author in Istanbul who runs a seven-translator review board.

Standing rule adopted: consult the actual Turkish critical literature and quote the translators from their own published statements. Do not invent an advisor-persona for a living translator whose work is under analysis.

2026-07-18 (second pass) — coverage check on three candidate NEW types

Purpose. A review found the dimension list structurally repetitive — ten of twelve were the same operation (extract, enumerate, tabulate). Before adding dimensions of genuinely different kind, check whether those kinds are occupied.

Money / re-costing the day — RULED OUT as a standalone dimension

Thoroughly covered, and more precisely than expected.

  • UlyssesGuide.com, "Money in Ulysses" — a dedicated resource converting 1904 currency to modern values, including Gifford's method of equating a 1904 penny to 1985 buying power via Thom's food staples, and a pint-of-Guinness exchange rate.
  • ulyssespages.blogspot.com, "Prices in 1904 Dublin" — a full price table across the novel with US2014 conversions and line references.
  • JJON, "liquidity" — analysis precise enough to determine which coins Bloom could physically have been holding, and how Gabler's emendation of the chocolate price from one penny to one shilling makes the Ithaca budget balance.
  • Bloom's budget itself is widely reproduced, and its known omissions (the 11s at Bella Cohen's, at least one tram fare) are documented.

Consequence: dimension 4, The Ledger, is retained on the list but flagged as duplicative. It is one of the six that the Type-I cap should drop.

Immersive / interactive recreation — RULED OUT in that form

  • Joycestick (Boston College, led by Prof. Joseph Nugent, public debut at the James Joyce Centre on Bloomsday 2017) — a VR game in which players move through scenes from the novel and gather 3D-modelled objects (Bloom's hat, Plumtree's Potted Meat, a phonograph), triggering sound and narration. Explicitly aimed at bringing new audiences to a famously difficult book.
  • JoyceWays — an earlier multimedia tour by the same team rendering nearly 100 Dublin locations with archival research, mapping and camera work.

Consequence: an immersive recreation would duplicate Joycestick, which is in effect the Object Museum in three dimensions. Dimension 13 is therefore specified with no representation at all — no streets, no rendered Dublin. A constraint graph, a clock and a purse. Different object, different claim.

Visual atlas — RULED IN

Ulysses has illustrated editions (Matisse, Motherwell) and a comics adaptation. All are illustration, which serves the text. A Warburg-method atlas — panels where meaning is produced by juxtaposition and the caption is refused — is a different form, and no atlas of this novel appears to exist. Adopted as dimension 14.

Standing rule adopted

A12 — Assume a subject is taken until checked. Of seven plausible "nobody has done this" candidates tested across both passes, five were occupied, two survived, and one survived only after being re-specified to avoid an existing project. Novelty claims go through source-research.md first.
