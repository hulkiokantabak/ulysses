Ulysses — The Fifteen Dimensions

Working document · build stage · v2 Base document 04. Revised 18 July 2026 after a review that found the first version structurally repetitive. Read this instead of v1.

THE REVIEW FINDING

The first version listed twelve dimensions. Ten of them were the same operation: extract a category, enumerate it, tabulate it, visualise it. Objects, women, money, bodies, the dead, songs, styles, schemas — the unit changes, the epistemic move does not. The three documents already built were that operation too.

Cortázar said this during the masters session — an inventory is not a dimension, it is a spreadsheet with ambition — and Galeano said his method destroys enumeration. Both were logged and neither was acted on.

A second finding, from the coverage survey, cuts the same way. Every content angle tested turned out to be taken. Smell, error, animals, accuracy against Thom's — and then money, which has a dedicated online resource and a liquidity analysis precise enough to determine which coins Bloom could physically have been holding. Even the immersive route is occupied: Boston College's Joycestick is a VR object-gathering game set in the novel's scenes, which is essentially the Object Museum in three dimensions.

Conclusion. Novelty will not come from subject. It has to come from form and standpoint. Dimensions 11 and 12 are strong not because nobody has thought about reader difficulty or Turkish translation, but because of who is doing the looking — one reader's actual first read, and a Turkish reader with four Turkish books.

So the dimensions are now organised by type of operation, and the additions are chosen to fill types the project does not yet have.

THE SEVEN TYPES

Type Operation Output is Dimensions
I · Inventory extract a category, enumerate, tabulate a reference 1–8, 10 (and all three built so far)
II · Inversion map the absence rather than the presence a reference of negatives 9
III · Case study primary evidence from one reader an argument from data 11
IV · Comparative the book against other versions of itself a parallel text 12
V · Model a constructed system you operate a thing that runs 13
VI · Made object a work, not a document about a work art 14
VII · Instrument a tool other people use to make data a dataset that grows 15

The standing recommendation, and it is the important one: build no more than three of the nine Type-I dimensions. Nine inventories is a spreadsheet collection, however good each one is. Better to build three excellently and spend the rest of the effort on types the project does not yet have.

Type II — Inversion

9 · The Concordance of Failures (panel −2, survives on Cortázar's inversion) Not the Homeric matches — the eighteen places where Joyce refuses Homer. Odysseus kills the suitors; Bloom gets into bed the wrong way round and asks for breakfast. Odysseus is known by his scar; Bloom is recognised by nobody, and the one unidentified man stays unidentified. Penelope holds out twenty years; Molly does not hold out one afternoon — and the book says yes to her anyway. One column, eighteen rows.

Type III — Case study

11 · The Difficulty Curve Where readers actually break, why, and what it costs to get through. The abandonment of Ulysses is globally famous and rests on almost nothing: the best-known metric is the "Hawking Index," invented in 2014 using Kindle highlight positions as a proxy and unscientific by its own framing; the claim that readers break at Proteus is classroom folklore attributed to no study. This project holds a complete dated log of one first reading with page-anchored questions at the exact points of strain and the interventions that resolved them. n = 1, stated in the document's own voice (anchor A10).

Type IV — Comparative

12 · Ulysses in Turkish Four Turkish versions — Belge's partial Penelope (1965), Erkmen (1996), Ekici (2012), Sevimay (2019) — set against the English on eight to twelve common cruxes. A live dispute, not a settled history: Ekici has written that Erkmen's strategies are part of why the book is still known to Turkish readers as cold and unreadable, and retranslated to recover its humour and humanity, working from Senn's warning about the lure of grammatical rectification. The comparative literature is one Brill chapter and one journal article; Sevimay is barely in it; almost nothing exists in English. Do not rank the four — the result is the trade-off structure.

Type IV — Comparative (second)

16 · FOUR ODYSSEYS

Homer, Wilson, Nolan, Joyce — measured on two axes that are usually collapsed into one. Material in hand: reading/Four_Odysseys_Handoff.pdf.

The source document's real contribution is not the four-way comparison but the frame that makes one possible: separating fidelity to Homer's plot and genre from empathetic and interior orientation. Collapsed into a single "which is more empathetic" scale, the distinction that matters disappears.

Work Fidelity to plot/genre Interior orientation Mechanism
Homer 100% — the source externalised; kleos and xenia oral epic; character through action
Wilson (2017) 100% — invents no plot high, without touching plot register — plain diction, refusal of archaism
Nolan (2026) ~70% — keeps genre, rewrites motive medium-high, by adding content adaptation — new scenes, reframed motive
Joyce (1922) ~0% — plot abandoned maximal, structural genre transformation — myth as scaffolding

The sentence that carries it: Wilson de-mystifies the sentence. Nolan de-mystifies the hero but keeps the myth. Joyce discards the myth's events and re-mystifies the ordinary.

And the finding worth arguing in public: Wilson and Joyce share an anti-mystification impulse — both remove something (archaism; heroic distance) rather than add. Nolan adds. That is three different kinds of empathy, and it is not the distinction most people make when they call an adaptation humane.

Why it pairs with dimension 9. The Concordance of Failures maps where Joyce refuses Homer. This supplies the measuring stick — refusing plot and refusing register are different acts. Build 16 first; it gives 9 its frame.

Evidence discipline, binding. The source was written one day after the film opened, from reviews rather than from the film. It marks its own confidence — High / Medium / Partial / Unconfirmed — and those markings survive into anything published (A5). Four questions stay open until checked: the Cattle of the Sun resolution, Calypso's memory arc, the Laestrygonian child, and whether Nolan has ever mentioned Joyce — nothing found.

Registers: 2 and 3, with the two-axis chart as the diagram (4).

Type V — Model

13 · THE CONSTRAINT MODEL

A toy model of the day as a routing-and-avoidance problem. You operate it; you do not read it.

Not a walkthrough and not a recreation. Joycestick already occupies that ground — a VR game where you gather modelled objects in the novel's scenes, which is the Object Museum rendered in three dimensions. This has no representation at all. No streets to look at, no Dublin to visit. A graph, a clock, a purse, and a set of obligations.

The model. Bloom starts with four shillings and ninepence. He has a funeral he must attend, an advertisement he must place, a wife he cannot go home to before four, and one man he must not meet. Stephen starts with three pounds twelve, no key, and no obligation at all. The city is a graph with real travel times taken from base document 03. The clock runs from eight in the morning.

What it reveals that a table cannot. Run it and the day's actual shape emerges: Bloom's day is governed by avoidance, not intention — nearly every route decision he makes is a decision not to be somewhere. Stephen's is governed by having surrendered a key — with nowhere to return to, every hour compounds. And the near-misses turn out not to be coincidences the novelist arranged but the expected outcome of two agents moving around a small city with overlapping errands. That last result is worth the whole exercise: it converts Joyce's most-praised contrivance into something structural.

Honesty rule. This is a toy model in the economist's sense. It is not a claim about what the characters felt or intended, and the document says so in its own voice.

Routes to: game-design-experts for the systems design; deep-thinkers if the model's claims need pressure-testing before publication. Registers: 1 above all. A short PDF explaining the model and its results (2). One diagram of the constraint graph (4).

Type VI — Made object

14 · THE ATLAS OF ONE DAY

Eighteen panels. Meaning made by juxtaposition rather than by argument.

Warburg's standing proposal, promoted from an open question to a dimension. The Mnemosyne Atlas arranged images on black panels so that relationships appeared without being asserted — no captions doing the thinking, no narrative sequence. One panel per episode: the place as it is now, the object, the newspaper page, the tram map, the handwriting, the schema entry, the photograph. No explanatory text on the panel itself.

Why it belongs here. Every other dimension produces a document about the book. This produces a work. It is also the only dimension that plays to the photographic and image-making side of this practice rather than the analytic side, and the Exposures series has already established the form.

Why it is not covered. Ulysses has illustrated editions (Matisse, Motherwell) and a comics adaptation. Illustration serves the text. An atlas does not — it argues visually and refuses the caption. Nobody has built one for this novel.

Routes to: exposure for the series discipline; visual-arts-experts for the panel review. Registers: 4 primarily, then 1 and 3. This is also the strongest candidate for the project's eventual physical form.

Type VII — Instrument

15 · THE READING LOG

Turn dimension 11 inside out: publish the instrument, not just the result.

Dimension 11's weakness is n = 1. The fix is not to apologise for it — it is to build the thing that makes n larger. A structured log any first reader can fill in: session date, pages covered, where they stalled, what they asked, what unblocked them, whether they stopped. Hosted, open, and designed so that completing it is easier than not.

Why this is the most valuable item on the list. If fifty people use it, the project owns the only real dataset on why the most famous unfinished book in the world goes unfinished — a question the entire guide industry is premised on and nobody has measured. It is the one dimension that could outlive the project, and the only one whose output grows after we stop working on it.

Discipline required. This is a study, so it follows study rules and not essay rules: pre-register what the log is testing before collecting anything; state the sampling bias plainly (self-selecting readers who found a website about Ulysses are not representative); publish the falsifiers alongside the findings; do not quietly redefine "stalled" after the data comes in. The study-discipline skill exists for exactly this and governs the dimension.

Registers: 1 is the instrument itself. 2 for each release of results. 3 once there is something to report.

METHODOLOGY

The dimension pipeline

  1. Declare the type before the unit. Which of the seven operations is this? A dimension that cannot name its type is probably a Type I in disguise.
  2. Declare the unit. The object, the woman, the crux, the panel, the constraint.
  3. Set the register budget. Not every dimension earns all four.
  4. Collect against the text, page- and episode-anchored, marking present / recalled / hallucinated.
  5. Separate sourced from inferred.
  6. Build the signature device — one per document, encoding something true.
  7. Audit — consistency sweep, then the panel, then the routed skill.
  8. Log — catches, learnings, bundle update.

Anchors

  • A1 Documentation before design.
  • A2 Scaffolding declared as scaffolding — Joyce printed no episode titles.
  • A3 Present, recalled, hallucinated stay distinct.
  • A4 Contradictions shown, not resolved.
  • A5 Sourced and inferred marked separately.
  • A6 The reader's own words stay as spoken, dictation slips included.
  • A7 One signature device per document.
  • A8 Registers budgeted, not defaulted.
  • A9 Nothing is finished until it is in the bundle.
  • A10 n = 1 is stated in the document's own voice, not a footnote.
  • A11 (new) Every dimension declares its type. No more than three Type I are built.
  • A12 Novelty is claimed only after a coverage check in source-research.md. Assume a subject is taken until checked.
  • A13 (new, 18 Jul) Repository shape. Standalone repo, private source, public site. Not a section of hulkiokantabak.com. Mirrored outside the single account from the first commit — the project's memory does not live in one place (Braudel).
  • A14 (new, 18 Jul) The URL contract is fixed before content lands and does not change afterwards. Private source with a public site means link permanence depends entirely on the publish step (Nelson).
  • A15 (new, 18 Jul) Dimension 15 does not ship before dimension 11 is complete. An instrument cannot be specified before the thing it measures is understood, and collection defects cannot be repaired retroactively (Ginzburg's binding veto, panel session 01).
  • A17 (new, 18 Jul) Build independence. This build uses only the author's own reading and this chair's work. Output from other models is deliberately excluded so a second, independent build can be compared against it later. An absence here is a decision, not a gap.
  • A16 WITHDRAWN by the author, 18 July. Structure is deferred with design — overruled. The anchor conflated two unknowns: which dimensions survive, and what kinds of thing the project contains. The second is settled. Architecture keyed to stable categories is safe; only architecture keyed to the dimension list would be fragile. Replaced by base document 05, 05-site-architecture.md.
  • A18 (new, 18 Jul) Architecture is not visual design. The site's structure — navigation, sections, URL contract, page templates, the dimension/register grid — is built now, at zero dimensions, because retrofitting it onto fifteen is expensive and building it at n=0 is nearly free. The look remains deferred under A1. Structure now, design later.

Working forms, before any build

  • Sketch — the signature device, drawn badly, in one minute.
  • Structure — the type, the unit, the fields, the count. Ten lines.
  • Register budget — decided and written down.

OPEN QUESTIONS FOR THE AUTHOR

  1. The Type-I cap. The chair recommends building three of the nine inventories and dropping the rest. This is the single most consequential proposal in this document. Accept, adjust the number, or reject.
  2. Sequencing. 11 → 15 → 1 → 13 → 12 → 14. The panel ranked 1 first.
  3. Dimension 15 makes the project a study, with obligations a set of documents does not carry — pre-registration, sampling honesty, published falsifiers. Confirm you want that commitment before it ships.
  4. Dimension 14 may be the project's real destination. If the atlas is the end point, the website and the presentation are staging posts rather than deliverables. Worth knowing early.
  5. Dimension 16 is now specified and its material is in the bundle. Confirm its place in the build order — the chair would put it immediately before dimension 9, since it supplies 9's analytic frame.
  6. One deferred idea, recorded not proposed: the book's method transplanted — one day, one city, this city. That is not a dimension of Ulysses; it is a work of your own that Ulysses would have caused. Noted here so it is not lost.
