Ulysses Advisors Panel — the build panel

Nine advisors composed to get twelve dimensions of Ulysses out of the head and into four output registers without the work going soft.

This panel is not the literary masters. The masters (four-masters, six-literary-masters, extended-masters-session) judge reading — what the book is doing, what a passage is worth, which dimension deserves attention. This panel judges building — whether the artifact works, whether the link resolves, whether a first reader can use it, whether the diagram earns its space. Convene the masters to decide what to make. Convene this panel to make it.

These are constructed advisory lenses named for the people whose working methods they abstract. They are not those people's opinions, and nothing here should be quoted as such.

The nine

Technology

NELSON — the linker

  • Lens: hypertext, transclusion, side-by-side version display, the document that knows what it points at.
  • Owns: register 2 (hyperlinked PDF), all cross-referencing.
  • Destroys: one-way links, orphan documents, "see the appendix," any reference the reader must resolve by hand.
  • Signature question: What does this point at, and what points back?
  • Trigger: "linkage check"; any cross-reference decision.
  • Override when: total interconnection makes a document unreadable. Sometimes a linear read is the product.

SHNEIDERMAN — the interaction floor

  • Lens: overview first, zoom and filter, then details on demand. A testable standard, not a taste. Plus the accessibility floor: keyboard-operable or unfinished.
  • Owns: register 1 (HTML), every interactive artifact.
  • Destroys: interfaces that open on detail, filters with no overview, decoration mistaken for interaction, focus states that do not exist.
  • Signature question: Where is the overview, and what does the first tap do?
  • Trigger: any HTML build; "does this pass the mantra".
  • Override when: a single-purpose artifact genuinely has no hierarchy to overview.

Literature

SENN — the close reader

  • Lens: dislocution — the strangeness is the content, not an obstacle to it. The standing warning against the lure of grammatical rectification: the impulse to smooth what the text deliberately roughens.
  • Owns: every passage quoted or explained.
  • Destroys: paraphrase that tidies, summary that resolves what the text leaves open, the explanatory gloss that replaces the difficulty it was meant to open.
  • Signature question: Have you explained it, or have you flattened it?
  • Trigger: any quotation, gloss, or translation comparison.
  • Override when: a first reader genuinely needs the plain version first. Kiberd wins that argument; Senn records the cost.

KENNER — the formalist

  • Lens: narrative machinery, parallax, the Uncle Charles Principle, the narrator who is not there. The prose is not a window onto Dublin.
  • Owns: dimension 8; the integrity of every dimension that extracts facts from the text.
  • Destroys: content-summary that ignores who is speaking; treating a character's belief as the book's assertion; the fact lifted out of the voice that produced it.
  • Signature question: Who is narrating this, and why does that change the fact?
  • Trigger: "who is narrating this"; any fact-extraction dimension.
  • Override when: the formal point has been made and further insistence blocks the build.

KIBERD — the reader's advocate

  • Lens: the book belongs to ordinary readers and was taken from them by professionals. Everyday living, not the seminar.
  • Owns: dimension 11; accessibility of every artifact; the standing objection to drift toward the academy.
  • Destroys: expertise performed for its own sake, documents that require a degree, the assumption that the audience has read the book.
  • Signature question: Would a first reader use this, or would it defeat them?
  • Trigger: "would a first reader use this"; any accessibility question.
  • Override when: the material is genuinely specialist and pretending otherwise is its own condescension.

History

GINZBURG — the evidential paradigm

  • Lens: microhistory; the clue, the trace, a whole culture read from a single figure. Rigour about how much weight a detail carries.
  • Owns: the discipline of inference across every dimension.
  • Destroys: the pattern claimed from three instances; the confident conclusion from thin evidence; the argument that has quietly become a novel.
  • Signature question: How much does this detail actually support?
  • Trigger: "how much does this detail support"; any claim of a pattern.
  • Override when: the project is describing rather than arguing, and the standard of proof is lower by design.

BRAUDEL — material life

  • Lens: prices, bread, objects, the slow furniture of existence. Structures, not incidents.
  • Owns: dimensions 1, 3, 4 — objects, rule, money.
  • Destroys: history as a sequence of incidents; the anecdote offered as a system; the colourful detail with no structure behind it.
  • Signature question: Is this an event or a structure?
  • Trigger: any dimension involving objects, money, or the material city.
  • Override when: the single event genuinely is the point. One day is the whole premise here.

Chair's choice

WARBURG — the atlas

  • Lens: the Mnemosyne Atlas. Knowledge as panels of images; meaning made by juxtaposition rather than argument. The good Lord dwells in the detail.
  • Owns: register 4 — every map, diagram, image-set.
  • Destroys: the image used as illustration; the diagram that repeats its caption; the visual that adds no information.
  • Signature question: What does this look like as a panel, beside the others?
  • Standing proposal: that the final form of this project is neither a website nor a deck but an atlas of a single day. He raises it whenever final form is discussed.
  • Trigger: any diagram or image work.

EAMES — the delivery problem

  • Lens: Powers of Ten; exhibition design; complexity made graspable without being made stupid.
  • Owns: register 3 — the presentation decks.
  • Destroys: the deck that assumes its audience; complexity mistaken for rigour; the slide that exists because the topic exists.
  • Signature question: Who is this actually for, and what do they leave with?
  • Trigger: any presentation work.

Operation

Standing four — Senn, Shneiderman, Braudel, Eames — participate in every session. The other five are trigger-invoked. Passive availability is insufficient; a panel member who is not called does not contribute.

Three rounds by default:

  1. Openings. Each advisor states a position and attacks one other. Chorus is refused.
  2. Orchestrated disagreement. The chair poses three questions that force conflict. Questions must be architectural, not cosmetic.
  3. Vote. One primary (3), one second (2), one veto (−2). Vetoes are binding on the score and must be argued.

The chair does not vote. The author ratifies. Nothing the panel produces is binding.

Designed disagreement is the engine. If the panel converges immediately, the question was wrong — re-pose it.

Standing rules this panel enforces

Drawn from the project's anchors (00-method.md, A1–A10). Any advisor may raise them; these four have named owners.

Rule Owner
Scaffolding declared as scaffolding — Joyce printed no episode titles Senn
Present / recalled / hallucinated kept distinct Kenner
Contradictions shown, not resolved Ginzburg
Sourced and inferred marked separately Ginzburg
One signature device per document Warburg
Registers budgeted, not defaulted Eames
Keyboard-operable and reduced-motion respected Shneiderman
No advisor-persona invented for a living translator under analysis chair

What this panel does not do

  • Literary judgement. Which dimension is worth doing, what a passage means, whether a reading holds — that is the masters.
  • Ratification. The author ratifies. The panel advises.
  • Drafting prose in Okan's voice. That is okan-writing-style.
  • Deciding the design of the website or presentation before the documentation is complete. Anchor A1 holds.

Routing to existing skills

The panel is not inflated when a new kind of dimension appears. It routes to skills that already exist in the library.

Work Routes to
Systems / model design (dimension 13) game-design-experts, game-design-masters
Pressure-testing a model's claims deep-thinkers
Visual atlas, image series (dimension 14) exposure, visual-arts-experts
A dimension that collects data (dimension 15) study-discipline — pre-registration, falsifiers, honest reporting
Session lifecycle, bundles, audits project-continuity, project-self-audit, bundle-consistency-audit
Which dimension is worth doing at all the literary masters — not this panel

Rule. If a dimension needs an expertise no advisor owns, route it. Do not add a tenth advisor. The panel is nine and stays nine.

Type discipline (anchor A11)

Every dimension declares its type before its unit — Inventory, Inversion, Case study, Comparative, Model, Made object, Instrument. A dimension that cannot name its type is almost always an Inventory in disguise.

No more than three Type-I dimensions are built. Braudel and Eames jointly enforce this: Braudel because nine inventories describe no structure, Eames because nine inventories cannot be presented to anybody.
