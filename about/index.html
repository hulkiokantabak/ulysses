About

The reading

One first cover-to-cover reading of James Joyce's Ulysses from 7th to 17th of July 2026, in eleven days and sixteen logged sessions on Claude (followed by parallel session in ChatGPT). Everything on this site descends from that reading and from the documents built while the book was fresh.

The reader is Hulki Okan Tabak.

The edition

Penguin Modern Classics, introduction and notes by Declan Kiberd (1922 text). The reference documents cite by episode rather than page, so they survive an edition change. Joyce printed no episode titles — Telemachus, Calypso and the rest come from schemas he circulated privately and then contradicted between versions; where this project uses them, it uses them as scaffolding and says so (anchor A2).

Rights note — read before publishing anything

The site is public. The repository is private, but everything that reaches the site is published. Three lines govern what may go up.

1. Ulysses (1922) is in the public domain. Quotation from the novel is unrestricted. Quote it freely and at length.

2. The apparatus around it is not. Declan Kiberd's introduction and notes in the Penguin Modern Classics edition are in copyright, as are Gifford's Ulysses Annotated, the Slote/Mamigonian/Turner Annotations, and every other secondary work this project cites. Paraphrase them in our own words and cite the source. Do not reproduce their text, and do not reconstruct a source's argument point by point in a way that substitutes for reading it.

3. Dimension 16's sources are reviews of a 2026 film. The film, its stills and its script are in copyright, and so is every review the comparison rests on. Paraphrase and cite; publish no stills without a licence; quote no review at length.

4. Emily Wilson's 2017 translation is in copyright (W.W. Norton). Dimension 16 compares it directly, which needs short parallel extracts — ordinary critical practice. Keep extracts brief, always name Wilson and the translation, and publish no continuous run of it. The same holds for any other translation the project quotes.

5. Commentary generated by other models is not our own writing. The documents in library/ were generated by a third model and supplied by the author. They are held as a cited source, never republished wholesale, and anything drawn from them is attributed to that source and marked for what it is — sourced fact, second-hand report, or the commentator's interpretation (anchor A5, catch C-23).

6. The PDF register embeds its typefaces, so the typefaces had to be free to embed. A downloadable PDF carries a subset of every font it renders, which makes typography a rights question rather than a taste question — the system serif this site uses on screen is a commercially licensed face, and embedding it in a public download is a licence question the project does not need to have. The PDFs are therefore set in DejaVu Serif and DejaVu Sans Mono, under the Bitstream Vera / DejaVu licence, which grants the right to reproduce and distribute the fonts — embedding included — on one condition: the copyright and permission notice travels with them. It does: design/fonts/LICENSE_DEJAVU.txt in the repository, and a line in every PDF's colophon. The fonts are build-time only and are never served by the site.

7. Dimension 14, the Atlas, is built from freely licensed material and nothing else. Rescoped by the author on 19 July 2026: publicly available sources only, and if a rights problem could not be avoided the dimension was to be struck. It can be avoided, so these are the rules it is built under (catch C-32).

  • Every image carries a named licence at a named URL on the credits page. No image enters the Atlas without one. "Probably old enough" is not a licence.
  • An institution's terms are not the copyright. The National Library of Ireland and Tailte Éireann both assert re-use conditions over their own scans while holding no copyright in the underlying works. Source the same material from a repository that imposes no such terms — Wikimedia Commons, per file. Do not source from nli.ie or its Flickr stream, whose terms are private-research-only.
  • Attribution lives on the credits page, never on a panel. CC BY 4.0 §3(a)(2) expressly permits this, and Creative Commons' own guidance names the case. There is a trap attached: CC BY-SA 2.0 requires credit at least as prominent as any other authorship credit that appears, so the colophon is lawful only while no panel carries any credit at all. Warburg's refusal of the caption and the licence compliance are one decision. Adding a single signature or caption to a panel would breach every share-alike licence on it simultaneously.
  • Do not crop share-alike images to fit the grid — pad them uncropped, which keeps the panels clear of the adaptation boundary. Licence the panels themselves CC BY-SA 4.0.
  • Cleared: period photographs attributed to Robert French (d. 1917; public domain in Ireland and the EU since 1988); contemporary photographs under CC0, CC BY or CC BY-SA, with Irish freedom of panorama (CRRA 2000 s.93) clearing the architecture; Thom's 1904 directory; the 1922 text.
  • Not cleared, and dropped: any page scan of a 16 June 1904 Dublin newspaper — no free source exists, and the digitisers hold the scans even though the 1904 text is out of copyright; Joyce's handwriting and any manuscript image — the Rosenbach expressly neither grants nor denies permission, and unpublished Joyce remains in copyright in the UK until 2039; photographic portraits of Joyce (Abbott and Freund both have active licensors); the separate tram map.
  • The map layer is settled, and the licence was read rather than inferred (19 July 2026, catch C-33). The Ordnance Survey six-inch Ireland sheets come from the National Library of Scotland, whose per-image tag on the Dublin sheets reads Re-use: CC-BY (NLS) and whose terms page resolves that to CC BY 4.0 — not NC, not ND, not SA. The required credit, verbatim: "Reproduced with the permission of the National Library of Scotland", plus a link to maps.nls.uk for online use. County Dublin is held in a revision surveyed 1906–1909 and printed 1909–1912, which is the layer this project wants. Two doors that look right and are not: the Digital Repository of Ireland applies its own CC BY-NC-ND reuse licence, which fails this bar, and links back to NLS anyway; and Wikimedia Commons holds no OS Ireland Dublin sheets at all. Go to NLS directly. Build constraint: the NLS IIIF server caps a request at roughly half a megapixel, so there is no whole-sheet download — the Atlas must tile at 512×512 or smaller.
  • The object layer cleared 17 of 18 (catch C-33), each with a named licence at a named URL. The panel that could not be cleared carries a period document in that slot instead, which is still a second register.
  • Where a photograph would mislead, use a document instead. 7 Eccles Street was demolished in 1967. In a caption-free atlas a convincing photograph of the surviving terrace would read as Bloom's door and be the wrong house, and there would be no caption to say so. The Thom's 1904 entry for the address is the honest element, and the better one.

Practical rule for the build: anything on the site that is not Joyce's 1922 text or our own writing carries an attribution line. If it can't be attributed, it doesn't go up.
