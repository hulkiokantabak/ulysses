The Difficulty Curve

DIMENSION 11 · TYPE III, CASE STUDY · THE UNIT: THE LOGGED QUESTION

This is a study of one reader: n = 1, an accompanied first cover-to-cover read of Ulysses, with every question asked along the way recorded verbatim, dictation slips included. Nothing here generalises. That is the point: the famous claims about where readers break rest on even less.

The primary record is the project's 117-page Reading Companion — sixteen dated exchanges, 8–17 July 2026, inside a reading of 7–17 July. From it, 245 questions could be placed against the book's eighteen episodes (290 were extracted in all; the placement rules and their costs are in the caveats, which are part of this document, not an appendix). Episode titles are schema scaffolding, not Joyce's — he printed none.

What the questions were made of

Nine kinds emerged from the questions themselves; each question carries one primary kind, so the counts are conservative. Two of the nine are not difficulty at all, and are kept visible because the curve is unreadable without them.

Kind (count)What it isIn the reader's words
Plot and orientation tracking (48)Losing or checking the thread of story, chronology, family relations, backstory delivered obliquely, and episode boundaries — including the standing end-of-exchange orientation requests ('help me understand... what awaits me') that were this reader's declared method of getting through the book.“I'm trying to understand: is Milly the wife of Leopold Bloom, or the daughter? Who is marrying whom?” PDF p.10
Allusion and period reference (47)Unrecognized references arriving unglossed: half-remembered songs, Shakespeare, Irish history and politics, Dublin places and institutions, 1904 objects and customs — the single largest difficulty class in the first half of the read.“While I praised specificity before, this level of specificity and time detail is making reading the story and understanding it 100 years later very tough.” PDF p.17
Style and form shifts (41)Each episode's new technique asked about on arrival — mock headlines, fugue prose, gigantism parodies, the history-of-English strata, playscript hallucination, catechism, the unpunctuated stream — plus section breaks, list devices, and nonsense language.“What are those sounds? How are these words created? There is also a mention of Miss Kennedy and Miss Douce. Who are these characters? What are we reading, and what chapter is this?” PDF p.31
Vocabulary and wordplay (25)Single words, idioms, slang, and puns needing a gloss — from 'public house' and 'cavalcade' to 'ribaldry,' 'shitbreeches,' 'halcyon days,' 'apropos,' and 'when the maggot takes him.'“Words that are tough: what does "ribaldry" mean? And "licentious"? I have an idea. What are we talking about here?” PDF p.55
Voice and referent confusion (22)Not knowing who is speaking or who is meant: interior monologue read as spoken dialogue, lines misattributed to the wrong character, half-asleep memory dissolves, and above all Molly's slippery unpunctuated pronouns in Penelope (7 of its 38 questions).“Who is she talking about? I just don't get it.” PDF p.110
Tone, stance, and significance (15)Uncertainty about attitude and weight: is Joyce (or Bloom) critical or affirmative, is a passage sarcastic, is a detail a planted symbol or 'just a random choice,' is this a joke or serious — including the reader's own false pattern-matches offered for testing.“That is a very strong reference to the Church being strong, but I wonder if it's affirmative on his part or critical.” PDF p.10
Felt but not grasped (6)The reader's signature strain: the music of a passage received while its logic withholds — beauty registered, meaning asked for wholesale. Undercounted at 6 primary assignments because its signature sentences usually wrap questions filed under other strains, and it recurs in state signals from Wandering Rocks to Ithaca.“This is all telling things I'm trying to understand. I get the feeling, but I don't get the main logic out of it.” PDF p.31
Dictation and transcription noise (6)Difficulty manufactured by the recording medium itself, kept as spoken per anchor A6: garbled names, pages, and quotes ('hate speech' for Hades, 'Lenin' for a swindler, 'unification' for micturition) that the answers had to decode before they could decode Joyce.“Okay, let's test the recording like this. Now we're reading more into the hate speech, and can you record properly?” PDF p.14
Curiosity and delight (not difficulty) (42)Not difficulty — kept visible because the curve has valleys: questions asked from pleasure, motif-spotting, and the reader's own readings offered for confirmation. Delight is the most common non-difficulty state in the record, densest in Penelope (12 of 38 questions) and around every episode ending.“What a fucking awesome paragraph!” PDF p.6

The hardest moments, in the reader's own words

“It's pretty impossible for me to read this book without your help, so I appreciate it.”

PDF p.40 · End of Exchange 8, closing the Cyclops day (episode 12) — The only outright impossibility statement in the whole record, made after a 90-page day through the end of Sirens and all of Cyclops; it names the condition of the entire read.

“Understanding this chapter is very hard, and the language is not that friendly or charming, at least to me. But I am surviving. I have got a mission to finish this book before I get to Copenhagen.”

PDF p.56 · Inside Oxen of the Sun (episode 14), Exchange 10 — The one episode the reader called hard and unfriendly in the same breath — difficulty without compensating charm — crossed on deadline discipline alone.

“This is very confusing for me to understand. I see the time reference, I see strange words, but what does that mean?”

PDF p.31 · The Sirens overture (episode 11), Exchange 7 — The sharpest single style wall of the read: three struggle signals cluster on this one pdf page as the fugue prose detaches words from sense.

“I am beyond understanding; I'm bewildered by the sound of it and the feeling of it.”

PDF p.30 · Exchange 7, Wandering Rocks into Sirens — Bewilderment stated at its maximum — and fused, in the same breath as 'This is an amazing passage. Amazing.', to delight: the record's clearest proof that this reader's difficulty and pleasure ran on one wire.

“What are we talking about? The times change, and everything moved from the scene by the sea into a room section. It is extremely much to ask.”

PDF p.48 · Nausicaa's fireworks scene (episode 13), Exchange 9 — Here the plot itself, not the prose, outran the reader: what actually happened on the beach had to be supplied whole, and the stopped watch had to be asked about twice before it landed.

What this one read shows

  1. The folklore that first readers break at episode 3 is contradicted by this one reader: he did not break at Proteus — he glided past it and hit his walls much later.

    Episodes 2–3 drew zero placed questions; one retrospective request covered both ('Now that the first three parts are over, I would like to hear more about chapters 2 and 3 as well,' pdf 6). His stated stance at the start: 'I'm confused. I'm not despairing. I don't mind. I'm gonna read gently until bloom appears in chapter four.' (pdf 4), and the Proteus beach drew 'What a fucking awesome paragraph!' (pdf 6). That is all this proves: this one reader, forewarned by a 'great introduction' and reading gently, did not break there.

  2. This reader's hardest sustained stretch was episodes 11–14, Sirens through Oxen of the Sun, where the style walls stack and the only survival language in the record appears.

    'I am beyond understanding; I'm bewildered by the sound of it and the feeling of it.' (pdf 30); 'This is very confusing for me to understand.' (pdf 31); 'It's pretty impossible for me to read this book without your help, so I appreciate it.' (pdf 40); 'Understanding this chapter is very hard, and the language is not that friendly or charming, at least to me. But I am surviving. I have got a mission to finish this book before I get to Copenhagen.' (pdf 56).

  3. Question count tracked engagement at least as much as difficulty: the three most-questioned episodes are the ones this reader loved loudest and read fastest.

    Circe (40), Penelope (38), and Ithaca (33) top the table; the same stretch holds 'Interestingly, today has been a 200-page read for me—very intensive.' (pdf 69), the catechism greeted as 'you might laugh at me, but it's a breath of fresh air' (pdf 80), 12 curiosity/delight questions in Penelope alone, and the breakthrough 'I'm living inside this day.' (pdf 81).

  4. The kind of difficulty migrated across the book: period reference dominated the front half, form the middle, voice and referent the end.

    Hades through Cyclops questions are dominated by allusion and Irish history ('this level of specificity and time detail is making reading the story and understanding it 100 years later very tough,' pdf 17); Sirens through Ithaca by each episode's new technique ('What sort of writing has this become? ... Once again, it changed a lot.' pdf 54); Penelope by Molly's pronouns ('Who is she talking about? I just don't get it.' pdf 110) — 7 of its 38 questions are referent questions.

  5. The difficulty curve recorded here is the curve of an accompanied read: the reader built a standing support structure and named it as load-bearing.

    Nearly every exchange ends with an orientation request ('Help me with what I have asked, and help me with what I have not asked.' pdf 81; 'as usual asking you what it means in general and what awaits me' pdf 63), and the dependency is stated outright: 'It's pretty impossible for me to read this book without your help, so I appreciate it.' (pdf 40).

  6. Feeling ran ahead of parsing throughout, and by the book's second half that gap became this reader's mode of access rather than his obstacle.

    'I get the feeling, but I don't get the main logic out of it.' (pdf 31) and 'I'm touching and seeing things that I can partly feel but cannot pinpoint.' (pdf 81) bracket the middle of the read; at Circe's close the same structure returns as breakthrough: 'I feel like I'm being shown things which on a conscious level I'm not able to perceive, but unconsciously I'm being attuned to.' (pdf 76).

  7. Some of the difficulty was not Joyce's but the microphone's: dictation garbles created questions of their own, and the answers had to translate the reader before translating the book.

    'Hades' arrived as 'hate speech' ('Now we're reading more into the hate speech, and can you record properly?' pdf 14); a swindler surfaced as 'Lenin' (pdf 47); micturition as 'unification' (pdf 92); 'Married to the crazy gnomes' garbled a barmaid's line (pdf 31). Six questions carry dictation noise as their primary strain and several more as secondary — per anchor A6, all kept as spoken.

Caveats — every way this can mislead

  • n = 1, always (anchor A10): one reader, one edition (Penguin, with the Declan Kiberd introduction he called 'very heavy, beautiful'), one accompanied first read, 7–17 July 2026. Nothing here generalizes to any other reader.
  • A question is not a unit of difficulty. The most-questioned episodes (Circe 40, Penelope 38, Ithaca 33) coincide with stated delight, breakthroughs, and the fastest reading; the least-questioned (Nestor and Proteus, 0 placed) were read before the exchange habit had settled. Counts measure conversation, not struggle.
  • The early book is undercounted: episodes 1–3 were partly read before the companion became a habit, and the single retrospective question covering episodes 2–3 (pdf 6) was left out of episode counts under the conservative-assignment rule — their zeros are an artifact of method plus timing, not proven ease.
  • The reader's words are dictated speech transcribed as spoken (anchor A6): names, page numbers, and quoted book passages are frequently garbled ('James Jones,' 'hate speech,' 'Lenin,' 'Married to the crazy gnomes,' '683' for 863). Quoted text inside questions cannot be used to reconstruct Joyce's text, and six questions exist primarily because of the medium.
  • Every answer shaped the next stretch of reading: episode keys, previews, and explicit difficulty warnings were supplied throughout, and the reader named the dependency ('It's pretty impossible for me to read this book without your help,' pdf 40). This is the curve of an accompanied read; an unaided curve would differ in unknowable ways.
  • Pace is a confound: the second half was read in much larger daily bites with fewer stops (120- and 200-page days; pages 689–789 in one day; the final 30–40 pages in one sitting), so later exchanges bundle more pages per question and per-page question density is not comparable across the book.
  • Episode assignment is conservative and sometimes follows exchange context rather than book pagination: 7 questions spanning episodes or addressing the book or edition as a whole were excluded from episode counts, and boundary placements (e.g., the Hades gramophone question asked inside the Aeolus-and-Lestrygonians exchange) follow the extractors' episode guesses.
  • Four extraction entries failed strict verbatim verification (pdf pages 10, 39, 109, 110 — trimmed lead-ins or silently elided words inside quotes). The underlying questions are real and their page anchors correct, so they remain in the counts, but none of their quoted forms is used as a peak quote in this dataset.
  • Taxonomy counts assign one primary strain per question although many questions carry two or more (allusion asked from delight, style confusion tangled with plot); the felt-but-not-grasped category is systematically undercounted at 6 because its signature sentences usually wrap questions filed under other strains. 38 duplicate extractions from adjacent slice overlaps were merged on near-identical verbatim; the twice-asked what-is-a-nation question (pdf 40 and pdf 47, across two days) was kept as two genuine askings.
  • Where the reader states ease or delight, that is data too: 42 curiosity/delight questions and dozens of delight state signals sit inside the book's reputedly hardest terrain — Scylla's prose, Circe's ending, Ithaca's catechism, and the final yes ('This "yes" is one amazing yes. It is actually one of the biggest yeses in literature,' pdf 111). The curve has valleys, and this reader's deepest valleys are late, not early.
